Competitive Swimming Strokes

Butterfly Backstroke Breaststroke Freestyle

NOTE: The following are the main 4 strokes of competitive swimming

  1. Butterfly
  2. Backstroke
  3. Breaststroke
  4. Freestyle
Short-Axis Butterfly Breaststroke
Long-Axis Backstroke Freestyle

Butterfly

Butterfly is one of the hardest strokes that a competitive swimmer can try to train for. It is a short axis stroke that has several upward and downward portions of the stroke that all are performed in sync to help propel the body forward. It is swum with both arms catching the water at the same time. It also ends with a propulsive kick from both legs and a propulsive finish of the arms that push the body slightly downward and yet forward at the same time. Breathing should be completed with as little effort as possible with the chin on the surface of the water per each breath taken.

Backstroke

Backstroke is an easier stroke to try to train for compared to the butterfly. However it still requires a lot of proper mechanics and work effort to have proper body position while maintaining forward propulsion. It is a long-axis stroke in which the arms and legs pull in a singular fashion. Being that this stroke is swum with more of a singular fashion the turn-over rate of the stroke is higher than the butterfly for sprints and the kick rate is higher for longer backstroke events. The swimmer is encouraged to keep the head back and not looking too far ahead or behind them. The body is encouraged to remain still and calm as the hands and feet rotate about the spine for maximum Distance Per Stroke or DPS. A tight streamlined position is preferred coming off of a start, turn, and going into a breakout.

Breaststroke

The breaststroke is a unique stroke in of itself as it is a very short axis stroke with several components to the stroke as a whole. The swimmer starts in a streamlined position then later moves the arms to an outsweep position to have an initial catch of the water. Then the warms have a swooping in motion to bring that water closer to the chest. Finally the arms shoot forward with propulsion from the kick to propel the body forward into the next stroke. The kick is what some call a froggy kick. The legs begin in a streamlined position and then also perform an in sweep to catch water. Then later the legs perform an out sweep to propel the water it has caught on the in sweep outward and behind the body to push the body forward into the next stroke.

Freestyle

Freestyle is one of the easiest strokes of the four that a swimmer can try to learn as it is easier for a swimmer to immitate an instructor on. It is a long axis stroke where the swimmer pulls with a single stroke and kick that are in sync with the other stroke to perform a smooth transition from one to the other. The swimmer begins the catch in a streamlined position and then pulls inward toward the belly button. Finally they drive the water from the belly button to the hip before lifting the arm elbow first to recover back to the catch position of the stroke. The kick is performed in a singular fashion and is in synch with each hip rotation that is incurred as the body is propelled forward per each stroke. The swimmer breathes on the side rather than the front as is done in butterfly.
